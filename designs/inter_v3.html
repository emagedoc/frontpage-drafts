CodeClash

Your LM is a good junior developer. But can it own the project?

Evaluating LMs as goal-driven software developers.

Goals, not tasks

No more explicit GitHub issues or specific tasks. Models must understand high-level objectives like maximizing income, territory control, or other scores—then figure out what to build.

Progress, not perfection

Models evolve their codebases across multiple rounds. They must analyze gigabytes of logs from previous rounds, adapt strategies, implement algorithms, and make all high- to low-level decisions.

Competition, not unittests

Models compete via their codebases in arenas where success is measured by performance scores or survival. Models must outthink and outcode their opponents in various languages.

A Far Cry from AGI

Outskilled by Humans

The worst human solution from the leaderboard still beats the best model by miles. When it comes to strategic thinking, humans remain far ahead.

Unable to Iterate

Models fail to analyze previous rounds for their shortcomings, hallucinate about failure reasons, and fail to improve meaningfully over time.

Decaying codebases

Models add features without removing old code, make random edits, and fail at long-term maintenance. Technical debt accumulates rapidly.

LMs have gotten pretty good at solving GitHub issues, but what if they have to find their own issues to solve?

Real software development isn't a series of isolated tasks. It's about understanding business objectives or higher order goals, making strategic tradeoffs, and evolving a codebase over time. It's about looking at competitor's performance and figuring out where you're falling behind. It's about knowing when to iterate on an algorithm and when to try a completely new strategy.

This is exactly what we want to address.

In CodeClash, language models build and evolve their own codebase over multiple rounds. Each round has two phases: edit, then compete. Models can write notes, analyze opponent logs, build and execute test suites, refactor code—whatever they think will help. Then their codebases face off in the arena. The output logs of the arena are copied back to the model's codebase and the next round begins. The winner of the tournament is the model that won the most rounds.
