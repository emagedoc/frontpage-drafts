Evaluating LMs as goal-driven software developers.

Goals Instead of Tasks

No more explicit GitHub issues or specific bug fixes. Models must understand high-level objectives like improving performance, increasing user retention, or winning competitions—then figure out what to build.

Iterative Development

Across multiple rounds, models maintain and evolve their codebases. They must analyze results, adapt strategies, refactor code, and make architectural decisions just like real developers.

Head-to-Head Competition

Codebases compete directly in arenas where success is measured by objectives like score maximization, resource acquisition, or survival. Models must outthink and outcode their opponents.

Key Findings

Gap to Human Performance

Despite impressive coding abilities, top-performing models lose every single round against expert human programmers. The gap between executing tasks and strategic thinking remains substantial.

Log Hallucination

Models frequently make confident decisions based on competition logs that don't exist or misinterpret results. They struggle to accurately analyze what actually happened in previous rounds.

Codebase Decay

Over time, codebases become progressively messier and more redundant. Models add features without removing old code, make random edits without proper analysis, and fail at long-term maintenance.

From Task Execution to Strategic Development

What's the difference between a junior and a lead software developer?
One thinks about how to implement, the other about what to implement.

LMs Excel at Tasks, Not Strategy

Language models have gotten remarkably good at solving GitHub issues. Given a clear bug report or a specific feature request, they can write quality code that passes tests. But what happens when they have to prioritize their own work? When they need to decide whether to refactor the authentication system, optimize the database queries, or add that new feature users have been requesting?

Real software development isn't a series of isolated tasks. It's about understanding business objectives, making strategic tradeoffs, and evolving a codebase over time. It's about looking at your competitor's performance and figuring out where you're falling behind. It's about maintaining code quality while racing to ship features. It's about knowing when to rewrite and when to iterate.

CodeClash evaluates exactly this capability. We give models a competitive objective—maximize your score, acquire more resources, or simply survive—and let them loose across multiple development rounds. They can write documentation, create test suites, analyze competition logs, or refactor their entire architecture. The question is: will they make the right choices?

The Results

Our results paint a fascinating picture. Models exhibit diverse development styles—some are aggressive optimizers, others cautious refactorers. But they all share fundamental limitations in strategic reasoning. They struggle to maintain clean codebases as complexity grows. They make decisions based on flawed analysis of competition results. And when faced with expert humans who can think multiple rounds ahead, they lose consistently.

The path from junior developer to tech lead is long and challenging. Our benchmark shows that for language models, this journey has only just begun.
